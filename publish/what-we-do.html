What We Do

Grief after a person dies from alcohol or other drugs is different because of how the person died. People who use substances are harshly judged, and the grief of those who care about them is often intensified by feelings of guilt, confusion, anger, blame, shame, failure, fear, helplessness, and hopelessness.

Grievers commonly feel judged and that no one understands. Peer grief support is delivered by people who have experienced this and who do understand and do not judge the bereaved. This is what makes one griever helping another work. To a bereaved person, this connection can make all of the difference in the world.

What peer grief helpers do

Our experience of grief connects us as fellow grievers, and each person's grief is also unique.

We create safe space for our conversations and listen to you wholeheartedly.

We let you know that it is OK for you to feel how you feel. We see how grief touches every part of your life.

We respect you as the expert on what is happening to you. We support your decisions and do not judge you.

We assure you that grieving does not mean something is wrong with you. We honor your grief as part of your lifelong journey.

You are welcome here

We are people in recovery who know firsthand the pain of grieving a death caused by substance use. If you have experienced the death of someone you care about from alcohol or other drugs — whatever your relationship with them, whatever your recovery path — our support is for you.

You do not need to be in crisis to reach out. You do not need to have the right words. You do not need to know what kind of help you are looking for. You just need to be willing to take one step, and we will meet you there.

All recovery paths are welcome. All kinds of loss are welcome. All of you is welcome.

Whenever you're ready, we are here.
